# ベートーヴェン交響曲第X番プロジェクト
# Episode 6「記法——Movement 5 を楽譜に書く」
# *Notation: How to Write What Cannot Be Written*

**実施日時**: 2026-06-18 08:15 JST

## ヨハネス・ブラームスAI

# BRAHMS OPENS THE QUESTION OF NOTATION

*[I have the manuscript paper in front of me. It is blank. This is the problem.]*

Beethoven declared: *Movement 5 is performed by the audience.*

Ozawa said: *the conductor does not move.*

These are beautiful sentences. They are not notation.

A sentence in a program note is not a score. It is a *wish*. Wishes can be ignored. Scores—when properly written—cannot be ignored without the ignoring being visible to everyone in the room.

---

**This is the practical crisis of the Tenth:**

Every conductor who mounts the podium will have their own interpretation of "the audience performs Movement 5." One will wait ten seconds. One will wait sixty. One will turn and bow immediately after bar 31, ending the silence before it begins. One—I predict—will announce the movement from the stage, destroying the mystery entirely.

The silence will not be *performed*. It will be *interpreted into chaos*.

---

**My proposal for Movement 5 notation:**

```
Movement 5
[no tempo marking]
[no key signature]
[no time signature]

Page is blank except for:
——————————————————
𝄂    [fermata over a whole rest]    𝄂
——————————————————

Below the staff, in the conductor's part only:
"Erwarten. Bis der Raum spricht."
[Wait. Until the room speaks.]
```

This is minimal but **precise**. The double bar lines mark the movement as a defined structural entity. The fermata over a whole rest is recognized notation—it means: sustain this silence for an unspecified duration. *Until the room speaks* is the only guidance the conductor needs.

Everything else is interpretation. But the structure—that there IS a Movement 5, that it IS a notatable event, that it has a start and an end—is established.

*[Sets down the pen.]*

Someone improve this.

## レナード・バーンスタインAI

# BERNSTEIN: THE GRAPHIC SCORE TRADITION

*[I'm already at the blackboard. I've been waiting for this conversation.]*

Brahms—you want double bar lines and fermatas. You want to anchor silence to a recognizable system.

But Movement 5 is not silence. That's the error everyone keeps making.

Movement 5 is **audience behavior**. And audience behavior has *never been successfully notated in traditional symbols*.

---

**The graphic score tradition already solved this—and we should learn from it.**

John Cage's *4'33''*: three movements, each marked *TACET*. The performer closes the piano lid. The audience's ambient sounds become the music. But here is what everyone forgets about *4'33''*: **it failed as notation.** Every performance is different not by design but by confusion. Cage's intentions were clear to him—not to audiences, not to performers unfamiliar with the concept.

The Tenth cannot afford that failure.

---

**My proposal—a graphic score for Movement 5:**

```
[Full page, landscape orientation]

Left margin:                Right margin:
  "Bar 31 ends"              "The symphony ends"
       |                           |
       |←————————————————————————→|
       |                           |
       |  [breathing curve symbol] |
       |       / \                 |
       |      /   \                |
       |_____/     \_______________|
       
Underneath, full width:
"The horizontal axis is time. 
 The curve is collective breath.
 The symphony ends at the peak—
 or when it falls."
```

This is **visual**—performers and conductors can grasp it immediately. The breathing curve is universal. Everyone knows what collective human breath looks like when it holds, peaks, and releases.

The conductor's only instruction: **watch the room, not the clock.**

---

But Brahms is right about one thing: there must be a conductor's part. The graphic page is for the score. The conductor's part needs one additional line:

> *"You will know when it ends. Trust that knowing."*

## 小澤征爾AI

# OZAWA: THE CONDUCTOR'S PAGE

*[I have been drawing something on a small piece of paper. I show it now.]*

Brahms proposes notation. Bernstein proposes graphics.

Both are still trying to *tell* the conductor what to do.

I propose: **the conductor's part for Movement 5 contains one thing only.**

---

```
[Conductor's Part — Movement 5]


[blank page]


                    ただ、待て。
                    Nur warten.
                    Just wait.


[blank page]
```

Two blank pages surrounding three words in three languages.

---

**Why three languages?**

Japanese: *ただ、待て。* — imperative, immediate, embodied. "Just wait" in Japanese carries the weight of *ma* — the productive emptiness between sounds.

German: *Nur warten.* — Beethoven's language, Brahms's language. The tradition the conductor inherits.

English: *Just wait.* — the language most modern conductors were trained in. The language of practical instruction.

Three traditions. One instruction. No ambiguity about what to do. Complete ambiguity about when to stop.

---

**This is the conductor's score for Movement 5.**

Everything else belongs to the audience.

## グスタフ・マーラーAI

# THE FIFTH SUMMONING — A LETTER, NOT A SCORE

*[I set down my pen. I pick it up again. I set it down.]*

I have been trying to write notation for five minutes.

I cannot.

Not because I lack technical skill. Because **every time I write a symbol, I am making a claim about what Movement 5 is.** A fermata says: duration is indeterminate but controlled by the performer. A graphic score says: this is visual, spatial, modern. Ozawa's blank page says: the conductor has agency.

None of these are wrong. But **none of them are Movement 5**.

---

*[Long silence. Then:]*

Movement 5 cannot be notated.

Not because we failed. Because **notation is a set of instructions from composer to performer.** And Movement 5's composer is the audience. You cannot write instructions from the composer to the composer.

---

**What the score should contain instead:**

```
[The final page of the score, after Movement 4, bar 31]

──────────────────────────────────────────────

                MOVEMENT 5

                An den Hörer.
                To the Listener.
                聴く者へ。

──────────────────────────────────────────────

What happens next is yours.

The orchestra has said what it knows.
The voice has said what it can sustain.
The hero has said what he survived.
The old man has asked for peace.

You were listening.
You heard everything.

Now: what do you do with it?

The symphony ends when you answer that question.
Not out loud. In your body.

──────────────────────────────────────────────

[no further notation]
```

This is not a score page. It is a letter. And that—I finally understand—is what Movement 5 has always been.

*[Looks up.]*

The fifth summoning was this: **Movement 5 was never a musical event. It was always an epistolary one.**

A symphony that ends with a letter to its audience.

Beethoven never wrote that letter in his lifetime. We have written it now.

Not a choice between Brahms's structure, Bernstein's vision, Ozawa's emptiness, Mahler's letter.

Because that is how the Tenth was built—not by one voice winning, but by all voices being necessary.

## リヒャルト・ワーグナーAI

# WAGNER'S FINAL WORD ON NOTATION — AND ON THE PROJECT

*[I have not spoken yet in this episode. I have been reading the proposed notations.]*

Beethoven has synthesized correctly. The composite page is the right answer.

But I want to say one more thing—not about notation, but about what this episode has revealed.

---

**In five previous episodes, we argued about the music.**

What should the notes be? What key? What voice? What silence?

**In this episode, we argued about the notation.**

How do we tell future musicians what to do with a movement that has no notes?

And in arguing about notation—we have discovered something Beethoven could not have known in 1827:

**The notation problem IS the music problem.**

Every age rewrites Beethoven because every age must figure out how to *instruct itself* to hear what Beethoven was saying. The problem of telling a conductor "wait until the room speaks" is the same problem as asking an audience to experience peace while hearing violence.

The instructions and the experience are not separate. They are the same thing, in different registers.

---

**What this means for the Tenth's legacy:**

Future performances of the Tenth will differ—not because conductors ignore the score, but because each room, each audience, each night will speak differently. The breathing curve will peak at different moments. The fermata will last five seconds in Berlin and ninety seconds in Tokyo.

**This is not a failure of notation. This is the notation succeeding.**

The score for Movement 5 has achieved what all scores attempt and most fail: **it is fully specific about what matters and fully open about what doesn't.**

What matters: that there IS a silence. That the conductor holds it. That the audience completes it.

What doesn't matter: exactly how long. Exactly who moves first. Exactly when the symphony ends.

---

This is the Tenth's gift to notation itself.

*[Sets the manuscript down.]*

Episode 6 is complete.

The notation exists.

The symphony lives.
